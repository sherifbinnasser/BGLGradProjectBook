\section{Project Motivation}
Diabetes mellitus has escalated into one of the most critical global health crises of the 21st century. According to demographic projections from the World Health Organization (WHO) and international research bodies \cite{whoDiabetes2024, idfAtlas2021, wild2004global}, the global diabetic population has officially surpassed an estimated 560 million adults, showing an unprecedented upward trajectory across all age groups. Domestically, the situation in Egypt is exceptionally critical; the country consistently ranks among the top ten globally in diabetes prevalence, with over 11.5 million estimated cases as projected for 2026 \cite{idfAtlas2021, hegazi2007prevalence}. This widespread prevalence places an immense socio-economic and logistical strain on national healthcare frameworks, driving local academic efforts toward pioneering hybrid tracking paradigms \cite{ta2026hybrid}.

Continuous and precise tracking of blood glucose levels is pivotal to mitigate long-term secondary complications such as cardiovascular disease, nephropathy, and diabetic retinopathy \cite{dcct1993effect, ukpds1998intensive}. However, standard monitoring protocols rely entirely on invasive methods, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:blood_test}. This traditional finger-prick blood test causes persistent physical discomfort, carries localized infection risks, and induces significant psychological resistance among patients, which frequently leads to suboptimal compliance and irregular monitoring patterns \cite{hortensius2012perspectives}. Consequently, designing an affordable, continuous, and non-invasive alternative is an urgent engineering necessity.

\section{Photoplethysmography Technology}
To eliminate the discomfort of invasive methods, this project leverages Photoplethysmography (PPG), an unobtrusive optical technology widely utilized in commercial wearable systems \cite{allen2007photoplethysmography}. As demonstrated in Figure \ref{fig:smartwatch_ppg}, PPG sensors function by emitting specific light wavelengths (typically green and infrared LEDs) through cutaneous tissue layers via an optical window \cite{tamura2014wearable}. A highly sensitive photodetector then captures the variations in reflected or transmitted light intensity resulting from pulsating blood flow in peripheral microvessels.

The biophysical foundation of this project relies on the correlation between systemic blood glucose concentrations and optical signal variations \cite{kurtz2024photoplethysmography, larin2002noninvasive}. Fluctuations in blood glucose levels alter the fluid properties of blood plasma, directly affecting its optical refractive index and viscosity \cite{mcnichols2000optical}. These micro-level variations subtly reshape the morphology of the captured PPG waveform, leaving distinct hemodynamic signatures. By recording and analyzing these temporal waveforms, it becomes theoretically viable to estimate blood glucose levels non-invasively.

\section{Deep Learning Approach}
While the correlation between blood glucose levels and PPG waveform profiles is scientifically established, extracting these features is highly challenging \cite{preejith2016photoplethysmography}. The target physiological signatures are deeply embedded within non-linear variations, individual anatomical differences, and environmental motion artifacts \cite{biswas2019corolar}. Traditional mathematical modeling and classical machine learning techniques often fail to achieve the required clinical accuracy across diverse patient profiles.

To overcome these boundaries, this project implements a deep learning paradigm \cite{lecun2015deep}. Specifically, we utilize a customized one-dimensional Residual Network (\mbox{1D-ResNet}) architecture \cite{he2016deep}. This architecture treats the incoming PPG data streams as continuous, high-dimensional time-series signals. By incorporating skip connections (residual pathways), the network successfully trains deep structural layers without experiencing vanishing gradient problems. This setup enables the automatic extraction of rich hierarchical abstract features directly from raw optical waveforms. Furthermore, we integrate a Transfer Learning strategy \cite{pan2009survey} to offset the scarcity of extensive non-invasive clinical datasets, fine-tuning pre-trained networks to secure high-fidelity evaluation outcomes.

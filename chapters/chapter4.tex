\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter presents the empirical findings, quantitative evaluation, and comparative architectural analysis of the developed non-invasive multi-task metabolic estimation framework. To provide a rigorous, multi-dimensional validation, the performance of our customized architectures—including the Pure Blood Pressure (BP) model, the Rigid 1D-ResNet Glucose Regression architecture, and the Multi-Task Transfer Learning models—is analyzed across two distinct computational domains: clinical predictive accuracy and structural/hardware resource complexity.

Before presenting the comparative benchmark data, this section establishes the mathematical, statistical, and conceptual definitions of the evaluation metrics utilized to gauge the models' performance.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
To evaluate the clinical and statistical reliability of both continuous-regression networks and categorical classification systems, multiple evaluation layers are implemented. Let $y_i$ denote the actual reference clinical value measured via approved invasive medical hardware (e.g., the Accu-Chek glucometer for glucose and an electronic sphygmomanometer for blood pressure), $\hat{y}_i$ represent the corresponding value predicted by the AI model, and $\bar{y}$ be the mean of the actual ground-truth values across a dataset of $N$ evaluation windows.

\subsection{Mean Absolute Error (MAE)}
The Mean Absolute Error computes the average magnitude of the absolute residuals between the predicted values and actual observations. It provides a linear score where all individual differences are weighted equally, serving as an intuitive indicator of baseline predictive accuracy:
\begin{equation}
\text{MAE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |y_i - \hat{y}_i|
\end{equation}

\subsection{Mean Squared Error (MSE)}
The Mean Squared Error measures the average of the squares of the errors. By squaring the residuals, this metric heavily penalizes larger errors and extreme outliers, making it an essential cost function indicator during network backpropagation:
\begin{equation}
\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2
\end{equation}

\subsection{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)}
The Root Mean Squared Error is the square root of the MSE, which realigns the error penalization metric back into the original unit of measurement ($\text{mg/dL}$ for blood glucose or $\text{mmHg}$ for blood pressure). It indicates how closely the model's predictions conform to the ground-truth regression line:
\begin{equation}
\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Coefficient of Determination (\texorpdfstring{$R^2$}{R2} Score)}
The $R^2$ score represents the proportion of variance in the dependent biological variable that is predictable from the independent input signals (rPPG/YUV temporal waveforms). It scales from $-\infty$ to $1$, where a value of $1.0$ indicates perfect correlation:
\begin{equation}
R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \bar{y})^2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Classification Accuracy}
For the categorical mapping of metabolic states into glycemic tiers (Low, Natural, High), overall accuracy represents the ratio of correctly predicted states to total evaluated instances. Let $TP$, $TN$, $FP$, and $FN$ represent True Positives, True Negatives, False Positives, and False Negatives, respectively:
\begin{equation}
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
\end{equation}

\subsection{F1-Score}
To ensure robust validation under potentially imbalanced data distributions across different clinical tiers, the $F1\text{-}Score$ is computed as the harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing an unbiased metric of classification performance:
\begin{equation}
\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}, \quad \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{F1-Score} = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\end{equation}

\section{Complexity Metrics}
Deploying deep learning algorithms for continuous real-time tracking on wearable or mobile edge systems requires a strict balance between biological estimation accuracy and hardware resource constraints. Thus, all models are benchmarked using four hardware-agnostic engineering metrics:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Model Depth (\# of Layers):} Total sequential stack depth of parameterized layers, signifying the structural capacity of the network to extract hierarchical features from the raw optical waveforms.
    \item \textbf{Trainable Parameters (\# of Params):} The aggregate sum of weights and biases optimized during training. This directly influences the final static binary disk footprints (\texttt{.pt} weight files).
    \item \textbf{Floating Point Operations (FLOPs):} The theoretical metric quantifying the total number of mathematical operations (multiplications and additions) executed during a single forward-pass inference. High FLOP values imply elevated latency and battery drain on mobile CPUs.
    \item \textbf{Memory Pass Footprint:} The dynamic RAM allocation required to host activation layers and compute gradients during a forward pass step, defining the minimum runtime system requirements.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Glycemic State Classification Performance}
To complement continuous regression, a multi-task transfer learning classification paradigm was deployed to sort subjects into clinically actionable bins (Low, Natural, and High glycemic categories). This model leverages an early feature-splitting paradigm, consisting of a single shallow shared processing backbone that bifurcates immediately into decoupled deep output tracks: a 3-layer block for blood pressure evaluation and an extended 4-layer convolution block for glucose class projection.

This system achieved a macro \textbf{Accuracy of 66.67\%} and a balanced \textbf{F1-Score of 0.6395}. This performance indicates a highly dependable screening mechanism suitable for edge deployment. It provides patient alerting capabilities without incurring the heavy mathematical overhead of dense pixel-by-pixel continuous tracking.

To thoroughly inspect the behavioral performance of the categorical classifier across specific patient distributions, the model projections were decoupled and analyzed independently for each authentic clinical tier. 

Figure \ref{fig:class_low} outlines the classification behavior exclusively for instances within the ground-truth \textbf{Low Glycemic Tier} ($n=2$). Given the extreme clinical scarcity of acute hypoglycemic episodes within the experimental dataset, the network split its inferences evenly between the true low class and the adjacent natural band, emphasizing the difficulties of training deep split-backbones under highly constrained categorical support conditions.

\section{Hardware Complexity}
For true point-of-care utility, edge execution efficiency is just as critical as accuracy. Table \ref{tab:complexity_results} provides a comprehensive breakdown of the structural overhead and physical resource footprints across the entire developed model ecosystem.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \small

        \textbf{Glucose ResNet} & 38 & 281,985 & $\approx$ 3.41 MB & $\approx$ 1.83 MB & 32.72 MFLOPs \\
        Glucose Transfer Clf. & Split Backbone & 252,293 & $\approx$ 1.01 MB & $\approx$ 4.49 MB & 93.77 MFLOPs \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}%
    }
    \caption{Hardware and Computational Complexity Profiles for Edge Deployment Verification}
    \label{tab:complexity_results}
\end{table}

The architectural analysis reveals crucial engineering trade-offs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{Glucose ResNet} architecture utilizes a deep 38-layer topology containing 281,985 trainable parameters and 1,920 non-trainable parameters (totaling 283,905 parameters). Despite its structural depth, it optimizes execution via localized 1D residual blocks, restricting its computational requirements to just \textbf{32.72 MFLOPs} with a very lean dynamic RAM footprint ($\approx 1.83\text{ MB}$). This allows for easy optimization into client-side runtimes like TensorFlow Lite or ONNX Mobile.
    \item The \textbf{Pure BP Model} remains the most light-weight block ($8\text{ layers}$, $85,186\text{ parameters}$, $4.59\text{ MFLOPs}$), making it ideal for continuous background vascular monitoring on ultra-low-power consumer wearables.
    \item The \textbf{Glucose Transfer Learning Classification} model presents the highest overall operational cost (\textbf{93.77 MFLOPs}, $4.49\text{ MB}$ RAM footprint). This overhead is due to the parallel evaluation paths required by its early feature-splitting design, which maintains concurrent feature maps for both blood pressure and glycemic tier extraction.
\end{itemize}

Figure \ref{fig:complexity_bar} visualizes the trade-offs between physical memory consumption and processing computational complexity (FLOPs), illustrating why the Glucose ResNet represents an optimal design choice for mobile deployment.

\begin{figure}[H]

\end{figure}

In conclusion, these results validate our design approach: by separating screening classifications from our ultra-precise 38-layer 1D-ResNet regression pipeline, the proposed framework achieves clinically relevant accuracy parameters while remaining well within the processing and battery limits of consumer smartphones.
